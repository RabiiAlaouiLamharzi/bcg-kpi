Design Annotations
Decision-by-decision rationale for every major design choice in the final dashboard
Layout and Structure
1
Overall Layout
Fixed sidebar with scrollable main content area
A management dashboard is navigated repeatedly across sessions. A persistent sidebar keeps the navigation always visible without taking up vertical real estate in the content area. The main content scrolls independently, which means charts and tables are never hidden behind a nav. The sidebar width is fixed at 232px, leaving the majority of the screen for data.

2
Page Header
Three-level header: eyebrow label, title, and subtitle
The eyebrow ("BCG Analysis") grounds the dashboard in its source context. The title names the view. The subtitle sets temporal scope without cluttering the title itself. This three-level pattern allows each line to carry a distinct type of information and creates a clean reading hierarchy before any data appears.
3
Section Headers
All-caps muted label with a trailing hairline rule
Section headers serve as chapter markers, not headings competing for attention. Using all-caps, muted grey text and a 1px rule keeps them structurally present without visual weight. A heavier treatment (such as serif bold or colored accent bars) was tried in an earlier iteration and consistently drew the eye away from the data it was meant to introduce.

KPI Cards
Four KPIs selected to cover market size, growth direction, spend shift, and risk
Each KPI was chosen because it requires a different type of response from the manager: monitoring, investing, renegotiating, or acting urgently. Together they give a complete picture in four numbers.

5
KPI Card Design
No colored backgrounds, no accent bars. Status communicated through text color and badge only.
Earlier iterations used filled colored badge backgrounds and colored bottom accent bars on each KPI card. These were removed because they created visual noise that competed with the chart section below. Status color applied to text alone (the badge value and the trend icon) is sufficient to communicate urgency without cluttering the card surface.

Data Visualization
6
Chart 1 — Storage Market by Type
Stacked bar chart with a 2:1 column ratio to the cloud chart
A stacked bar was chosen over a grouped bar because the manager needs to see both the total market size and the composition of that total simultaneously. Grouped bars would require the eye to compare across groups to estimate totals. The 2:1 width ratio reflects that this chart carries more information and context than the cloud adoption chart beside it.

7
Chart 2 — Public Cloud Spend
Area line chart with amber color to signal a monitoring situation
The line chart with a light fill communicates a trend over time clearly for a single metric. Amber was chosen rather than green or red because cloud growth is not inherently good or bad for the manager — it is a shift that requires attention and strategic response. The color nudges the reader toward watchfulness rather than alarm or celebration.
8
Chart 3 — Data vs Capacity
Dual Y-axis line chart using red for data creation and blue for storage capacity
The two metrics (data created in zettabytes vs storage capacity in zettabytes) have vastly different scales, making a shared axis misleading. A dual axis was used intentionally, with each axis label color-matched to its line so the reader does not need a legend to understand which axis belongs to which series. Red signals risk (the growing gap), blue signals the resource that needs to keep up.

9
Chart 4 — File vs Object Storage
Grouped bar chart showing the two segments side by side
A grouped bar rather than a stacked bar was chosen here because the message is about the relative growth of two competing segments, not their combined total. The subtitle states the conclusion directly ("Object is growing at nearly three times the rate of File") so the chart becomes evidence for a decision rather than a data display.
10
Chart Card Header
Card header includes a bottom border separating metadata from the chart canvas
The title, subtitle, and legend are information about the chart. The canvas is the chart itself. A 1px separator between the two makes the card feel well-organized and prevents the legend from visually blending into the chart area. This also creates a consistent reading pattern: read the heading to understand what you are looking at, then look at the chart.
Alert Banner and Insight Cards
11
Alert Banner
Placed above KPIs, white background, red left border only
The alert is the first thing a manager should see if something requires action. Placing it above the KPI row ensures it is read before any data. A white background with a 3px red left border communicates urgency without being visually overwhelming — it is a signal, not an alarm. The alert text is written as a plain statement of risk followed by a concrete recommendation.

12
Insight Cards — "What This Means for You"
Three insight cards placed after charts, with colored top border indicating priority
Charts show what is happening. Insight cards tell the manager what to do about it. Placing them after the charts means the reader has context before reaching the recommendations. The 3px top border encodes priority at a glance: red for immediate action, green for opportunity, amber for ongoing monitoring. Each card ends with a short priority tag that reinforces this reading.

Typography and Color System
13
Typography
Two typefaces only: Libre Baskerville for display, IBM Plex Sans for UI
Libre Baskerville is used exclusively for elements that carry the most important information: the page title, card titles, and KPI metric values. Its serif character signals authority and makes large numbers more legible at a glance. IBM Plex Sans handles everything else. This strict two-typeface rule prevents visual fragmentation and gives each typeface a clear role.

14
Color System
Neutral background, one brand green, three semantic status colors
Color is used for meaning only, not decoration. The neutral grey background (#f6f6f6) ensures white cards stand out cleanly without any warm or cool bias. The brand green (#177A47) appears in active nav states, the logo, and positive indicators. Red, amber, and blue-purple are reserved for status encoding only and appear consistently in the same roles across charts, KPIs, pills, and insight cards.

15
Table Design
No card wrapper. Table has its own outer border and owns its container.
The segment comparison table is the densest component on the page and benefits from being clearly bounded. Rather than floating inside a generic card, it has its own 1px border with a border-radius, a slightly shaded header row, and consistent left padding matching the rest of the page. The inline bar spark in the "Share by 2025" column adds a visual dimension to the percentage without requiring a separate chart.
